Inductors are passive electronic components used in electrical circuits to store and control energy in the form of a magnetic field. They consist of a coil of wire wound around a core, typically made of materials like iron or ferrite. When current flows through an inductor, it generates a magnetic field around the coil. The key property of an inductor is its inductance, measured in henrys (H), which determines its ability to resist changes in current flow. Inductors are commonly used in circuits to filter out high-frequency signals, store energy in power supplies, and provide impedance in AC (alternating current) circuits. They play a crucial role in various applications, including transformers, electric motors, and filters, and are essential components in many electronic devices.
